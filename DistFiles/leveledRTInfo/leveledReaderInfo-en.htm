Predictability

Predictability in a text means that a text has repetition of specific language, the context is familiar to the reader, pictures are supportive to the text, language patterns are common to readers, often there is rhyme, and the reader can guess/predict what would come next in the storyline.

Some criteria for predictable books are:

  • Pattern and recognition (Predictable text)

    • Repetition - a story with one sentence or refrain that is repeated
    • Sequencing - a story with a known sequence such as the days of the week or the use of numbers in a pattern
    • Building Sequence - a story with a pattern that is repeated and added to with each new page
    • Rhyme - a story with a pattern or sequence that also includes rhyme 
      Brown Bear, Brown Bear, What do you see?
I see a red bird looking at me.
Red Bird, Red Bird, What do you see?
I see a yellow duck looking at me.
Yellow Duck, Yellow Duck, What do you see?
      - Bill Martin, Jr. and Eric Carle

  • Illustration support

    • When choosing illustrations to go with your story, choose pictures that closely match your story line. The reader should be able to predict the story line just by looking at the picture.

    Read more about illustration support online

  • Concept load (Topic familiarity)

    • When developing a book choose topics that are familiar to the readers. Readers will be eager to read and will read more if they are reading about things they are interested in and familiar with.
    • For Emergent Readers the text and concepts should be highly supported by pictures. Concepts should be concrete, should focus on one idea/theme and should be easy to understand.
    • For Early Readers develop a story around one familiar concept with less support by pictures. As readers become more fluent, develop a story around a familiar concept but in greater depth.
    • For Fluent Readers the concepts are expand beyond what is familiar, are more varied and abstract.
